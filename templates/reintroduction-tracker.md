## ⚙️ THE PROTOCOL IN ONE PARAGRAPH

Reintroduce **ONE food group at a time.** On Day 1, eat a moderate serving of the test food 1–3 times that day. On Days 2–3, continue eating it or stop — depending on reaction. Then **return to a clean baseline for 2–3 days** before testing the next food. Keep a symptom log for 0–72 hours after each trial: bloating, gas, reflux, skin, sleep, mood, energy, joint pain, headaches, brain fog, bowel changes.

**Why one at a time?** If you test two foods on the same day and have a reaction, you can't tell which food caused it. You lose the whole data point.

## 🔢 SUGGESTED REINTRODUCTION ORDER

Ordered from least-likely-to-react to most-likely-to-react. **Your practitioner may have a different recommended order — defer to them if so.**

| # | Food Group | Example test foods | Why this slot |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Eggs | Whole eggs, cooked plain | Nutrient-dense, moderate reactivity |
| 2 | Dairy — goat/sheep first, then cow | Goat cheese → cow yogurt → cow milk | Goat/sheep often tolerated when cow isn't |
| 3 | Corn | Plain cooked corn, corn tortilla | Moderately reactive; test before gluten |
| 4 | Gluten-containing grains | Sourdough, pasta, oats (if excluded) | High reactivity for many — full 3-day window |
| 5 | Soy (whole-food form) | Edamame, tempeh, tofu — not refined oil | Test as whole food for real read |
| 6 | Peanuts & other legumes | Peanut butter, lentils, chickpeas | Test separately from soy |
| 7 | Red meat (if excluded) | Grass-fed beef, lamb | Usually tolerated once gut is calm |
| 8 | Shellfish (if excluded) | Shrimp, scallops | Lower priority unless you eat it often |
| 9 | Added sugar | Dessert, sweetened drink | Save for later — easy to over-test |
| 10 | Alcohol | One serving of one type | Stresses liver post-detox |
| 11 | Caffeine (if excluded) | Coffee or black tea | Last because it masks fatigue |

## 🚦 SYMPTOMS TO WATCH FOR (0–72 hours after each trial)

- **Digestive:** bloating, gas, reflux, nausea, constipation, diarrhea, stomach pain
- **Skin:** acne, eczema, itching, rosacea, rashes
- **Energy/brain:** fatigue, brain fog, mood dip, anxiety spike, irritability
- **Sleep:** trouble falling asleep, waking at 2–4 AM, non-restorative sleep
- **Joints/body:** joint pain, stiffness, headaches, muscle aches
- **Endometriosis / pelvic:** pelvic pain increase, cramping, cycle changes, heavier bleeding

**For endometriosis:** dairy, gluten, soy, and alcohol are the four foods most commonly implicated in endo flares per the research. Extra attention to those trials.

## 🧠 COMMON PITFALLS

1. **Testing two things at once** — one reaction becomes unattributable
2. **Testing during a stressful event** — cortisol produces symptoms that look like food reactions
3. **Only testing a refined version of a food** (e.g., soy oil instead of whole-food soy)
4. **Not journaling** — reactions 48–72h out are easy to dismiss without a written baseline
5. **Shame-spiraling over a reaction** — a "fail" is *data*, not a character defect
6. **Stopping the trial after 1 day of no reaction** — give it 72 hours
7. **Skipping clean baseline days between trials** — you need quiet signal to hear the next food

## 💡 PARALLEL TRACK SUGGESTIONS

- **Loop in your practitioner** before Trial 1 — a 10-minute message asking for adjustments to the order based on your history is high-leverage
- **Photo-log meals** during trials — 5 seconds, creates a visual trail for pattern-finding later
- **WHOOP / Garmin correlation:** HRV and sleep often move 24–48h BEFORE you consciously feel a reaction — objective signal during the "sneaky symptom" window
- **Sweat therapy (infrared sauna or similar):** supports the liver between trials by mobilizing fat-soluble toxins via the skin. Worth considering for anyone doing a structured reintroduction after a detox

---

## 🎯 GOAL OF REINTRODUCTION

Not: "go back to eating everything."
Yes: **build a personalized food map of what genuinely supports your body vs. what costs you energy, sleep, or pain.** By the end of reintroduction, you'll know — with evidence, not guessing — which foods are background-safe, which are occasional-only, and which are worth holding out from long-term.
